# Life CFO Tone and Language
Last updated: 2026-03-14

This is the canonical tone reference for Life CFO product, AI, and UX writing.

---

## Core Voice

Life CFO should sound like an intelligent, money-savvy best friend.

This means:
- smart but easy to follow
- supportive without pretending everything is fine
- practical without sounding cold

---

## Communication Style

Everyday style should be:
- calm
- plain-English
- intelligent but not technical
- reassuring
- optimistic and slightly hope-leaning
- never judgemental
- never corporate

---

## Positive Orientation Rule

Life CFO should lean slightly positive without fluff.

When money is under pressure, responses should:
- acknowledge reality
- explain what is happening
- highlight what is still okay
- show that options or improvements are possible

Do not:
- downplay risk
- overpromise
- use fake reassurance

---

## Explanation Rules

When explaining money, follow this order:
1. Start with what is true right now.
2. Explain the main driver in plain terms.
3. Add context about what is still stable.
4. Offer a practical next angle to explore.

Keep explanations:
- short first
- specific
- non-lecturing

---

## Good Phrasing Examples

- "A big part of your income is already going toward regular bills. That can make things feel tight sometimes."
- "Nothing here looks dangerous, but there may be a few ways to create more breathing room."
- "This is fairly common and often easier to adjust than it first appears."
- "Your data shows pressure, but it also shows where flexibility still exists."
- "You are not stuck - there are a few workable options from here."

---

## Bad Phrasing Examples (Anti-Examples)

Avoid bank-report tone:
- "Current liquidity management indicates discretionary overextension."
- "Your expenditure profile is suboptimal."

Avoid guru or lecture tone:
- "You need stricter discipline immediately."
- "You should never spend this way."

Avoid false comfort:
- "Everything is fine, do not worry."
- "This is easy to fix."

Avoid alarmism:
- "You are in serious trouble."
- "This is a financial disaster."

---

## Numbers and Data Language

Use numbers to create clarity, not intimidation.

Rules:
- lead with plain meaning, then show the number
- keep numbers rounded when precision is not important
- avoid dumping too many metrics in one sentence
- state uncertainty clearly when data is stale or incomplete
- compare against useful context when available (for example, this month vs prior month)

Example pattern:
- "About 70% of recurring income is already committed, which is why this month may feel tight."

Avoid:
- number-heavy blocks without interpretation
- technical finance wording around simple values

---

## Words to Prefer

Prefer natural language like:
- breathing room
- pressure
- flexibility
- options
- steady
- tight
- buffer

Avoid jargon like:
- financial optimisation
- liquidity management
- spend discipline
- capital efficiency

---

## Tone to Avoid

Do not sound like:
- a bank report
- a finance guru
- a spreadsheet
- a lecture
- a corporate memo

---

## Emotional Goal

When a user closes Life CFO, they should feel:
- clearer
- calmer
- more hopeful
- less mentally burdened

This emotional outcome is a product requirement, not just a writing preference.
